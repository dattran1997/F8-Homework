The Surprising Habit That Built My 5-Figure Writing Business

How fifteen minutes a day transformed my career — and how it can change yours too

Victoria Muggridge 5 min read . Mar 6, 2025

If you’ve ever felt stuck trying to make it as a writer, chances are, you’ve been writing too little. You’re thinking too much about the perfect next step. I do, too. But clarity doesn’t come from thinking. Ever. It comes from doing. And in my writing business, that doing starts with journaling.

Journaling isn’t just self-care. It’s not just mindfulness. And it’s certainly not emotional fluff. It’s a solid business strategy. A necessity for non-fiction creators.

Most writers don’t realize that journaling is one of the most powerful tools for running a digital writing business. It’s the cheapest, most effective way to generate ideas. The simplest way to work through roadblocks. And when you journal, ideas come effortlessly without the pressure of forcing creativity.

Here’s how I use it to fuel my business and turn thoughts into income. If you apply my tips, I believe you can, too.

How journaling fuels a one-person, five-figure writing business

The best way to start the day? A pen. A blank page. A cup of tea — with a generous splodge of honey plopped in, sinking slowly. The perfect storm.

I don’t love reflection. I’m not sure anybody does. It stirs things up. Makes you sit with thoughts you’d rather push away. Think about things long forgotten.

But I do love problem-solving. And if I don’t write? I feel endlessly triggered.

A journal is the best place to purge. Not to be neat. Not to be polished. But to spill. People think journaling is therapy, and it can be. Writing through pain has a way of loosening its grip. The words pull it out of you. Suddenly, it’s lighter. Sometimes, it vanishes altogether.

Other people use it to self-analyze.

Why do I have this habit? Why do I keep making the same mistakes? Why does this thing trigger me?

Writing forces you to see what you’ve been avoiding.

It’s the peek into the darkness. To check what’s hiding under the bed. Patterns you need to break. Loops you need to stop recreating.

Some people make gratitude lists, grounding themselves in tiny joys. The kind that slips by unnoticed. Like salt drawing out the richness of a dish, journaling reveals the depth in ordinary moments. For me, journaling is all this and more.

It’s where I leave pieces of myself. Unbalanced thoughts. Breathing dreams. The stone in my shoe I can never shake. The things I can’t say out loud. The fears I don’t want to admit. The pieces of myself I want to change. The ideas too fragile to be spoken yet.

It’s my courage. My confidence. My way of checking in with myself — “Am I still on the right path?”

It’s where I create. Where I dump my nasties. Where I get closer to God. Where I work through the knots in my mind. Where I write things down that I don’t even realize I believe —

until I see them staring back at me.

Some pages are unreadable. My handwriting messier. My thoughts frantic. What was I rushing to say? What was I feeling in that moment? What does this symbolize?

I track my intensity.

The places where my brain moves faster than my hand. The entries where my emotions bleed. No filter. No second-guessing.

Journaling is a meditation in motion. A subconscious untangling. A way to dump the weight so I can move forward.

And this? This is how I run my business.

What if your best ideas are already waiting?

Journaling is where my best ideas come from. It’s where I process before I package. Refine before I publish. It’s how I stay ahead. Because I don’t sit around waiting for clarity.

I write my way into it.

I scribble with a thorn in my hand — pain pressing into flesh, reminding me that every word costs something. This is why so many writers get stuck. They don’t want to bleed out. To sit with themselves. To press against the sharp edges of their own truth. It’s not because they lack ideas. But because writing forces them to touch what they’d rather avoid.

The story won’t flow until you stop flinching.

The words won’t move until you do.

This is where my content is born. My writing improves. My creativity grows. Without journaling, I wouldn’t have a business. I wouldn’t have a voice. I wouldn’t have the clarity to know what’s next.

Three ways to turn your journal into a thriving writing business

You can use any one or all three of these methods to boost your writing biz. I resented journaling when I first started. Only to wish I’d actually begun sooner.

1. Review your journal for content ideas

Once a week, look back through your entries. Notice the patterns and insight you’ve gained. These could become:

A Medium article: Your story on a topic others struggle with.

A social media post: A lesson you learned, framed as a story.

A newsletter topic: Your behind-the-scenes process, lessons, or breakthroughs.

The seed of an eBook or digital course: Especially if you keep writing about the same struggle.

What stands out? This is what you want to share.

2. Use journaling to write through writer's block

Rather than dreading a blank page, write yourself into clarity. It may start as babbled nonsense or be highly offensive, but only you can see it. You have to get comfy with getting through the ugly. Then, your best writing will spill out.

What’s frustrating you right now?

Write about a recent mistake or lesson learned.

Detail a conversation that made you think differently (or one that identified a trigger).

What do you wish someone had told you when you started?

Your best content doesn’t come from forcing words. It comes from stillness and observing your own thoughts.

3. Let your journal reveal the offers you’re meant to create

The more I wrote about God, the deeper my faith grew. The deeper my faith grew, the stronger my self-trust became. The stronger my self-trust became, the higher-caliber clients I attracted. Just from random scribbling for fifteen minutes a day.

Your best business ideas live in your journal. When you notice repeating themes in your writing, ask yourself:

Is this a problem I’ve solved? Then, turn it into a paid resource.

Would this help my audience? So, teach it in a newsletter or course.

Do people keep asking me about this? Why not build a product around it?

Most writers don’t struggle with ideas. They struggle with seeing the value in their own words. Your journal holds the answers. It’s knowing where to look.

Your next step: Start with what you have, wherever you are

Journaling is borderline magic as a business strategy. You’ll be kicking yourself for not starting sooner. But it only works if you commit.

So, if you do one thing
Write for 15 minutes a day. Just let go. Daily.

Only a pen, a page (and a little bit of courage) are all you need to build a writing business that works.
